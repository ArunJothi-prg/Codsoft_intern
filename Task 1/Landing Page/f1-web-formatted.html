About

Formula One, also called F1 in short, is an international auto racing sport. F1 is the highest level of single-seat, open-wheel and open-cockpit professional motor racing contest.
Formula One racing is governed and sanctioned by a world body called the FIA − Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile or the International Automobile Federation. The name ‘Formula’ comes from the set of rules that the participating cars and drivers must follow.

The objective of a Formula 1 contest is to determine the winner of a race. The driver who crosses the finish line first after completing a pre-determined number of laps respective of team strategies is declared the winner.

“Formula 1 is not just about the race, it is made up of rules and regulations”

The rules and regulations of Formula 1 (F1) racing are comprehensive and constantly evolving. However, here's a broad overview of some of the key rules governing F1:

1. Technical Regulations:
These cover specifications for the cars including dimensions, weight, engine, gearbox, aerodynamics, safety features, and more. The FIA (Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile) regularly updates these regulations to maintain competitiveness and safety.

2. Sporting Regulations:
These govern the conduct of races, including qualifying sessions, race procedures, flags and signals, pit stops, overtaking, penalties, and more.

3. Points System:
Points are awarded to drivers and teams based on their finishing positions in races, with the winner receiving the most points. The current system typically awards points to the top 10 finishers.

4. Race Weekend Format:
A typical race weekend includes practice sessions on Friday, followed by further practice and qualifying sessions on Saturday to determine the starting grid for the race on Sunday.

5. Technical Scrutineering:
Before and after each race, cars undergo scrutineering to ensure compliance with technical regulations. Any violations can result in penalties.

6. Parc Fermé:
After qualifying and before the race, cars are placed in parc fermé conditions where no significant changes can be made to them until the race starts.

7. Safety Car:
In the event of an incident or unsafe conditions on track, the Safety Car may be deployed to slow down the field and maintain safety.

8. DRS (Drag Reduction System):
This is a system that allows drivers to adjust the rear wing of their car to reduce drag and increase straight-line speed, aiding overtaking.

9. Tyre Regulations:
F1 uses multiple tyre compounds provided by a single supplier for each race. Teams must use at least two different compounds during a race, and there are restrictions on the number of tyres each driver can use over a race weekend.

10. Budget Caps:
Starting from the 2021 season, F1 introduced budget caps to control the spending of teams in an attempt to level the playing field.

11. Driver and Team Conduct:
F1 imposes codes of conduct on drivers and teams regarding on-track behavior, interactions with officials, media obligations, and more.

12. Testing Regulations:
Limitations on testing outside of official test sessions are in place to control costs and maintain fairness among teams.

These are just some of the many rules and regulations that govern Formula 1 racing. The FIA periodically updates these rules to ensure the sport remains competitive, entertaining, and safe.

Weekend Structure

The standard formula 1 Grand Prix weekend event takes place over three days from Friday to Sunday.

FREE PRACTICE

Friday - Free Practice sessions 1 & 2 (FP1 & FP2) ~ 1 hour/each
Saturday - Free Practice session 3 (FP3) ~ 1 hour

After this final hour-long practice session gives the teams more time to fine-tune their set-ups before parc ferme conditions begin and most work on the car is banned.

QUALIFYING

Saturday (afternoon), three stages –> Q1, Q2 and Q3 – which last 18 minutes, 15 minutes and 12 minutes respectively.

The five slowest drivers are eliminated after Q1, before five more drop out from Q2 – thereby setting the grid positions from 20th up to 11th.

The ten remaining drivers head into Q3 to determine the top 10 grid slots, with the fastest driver taking pole position for Sunday's race.

F1 GRAND PRIX

Sunday - Final race ~ upto 2 hours

After the final race top 10  players & teams are  awarded with championship points and with an extra point given to the driver and the team with the fastest lap of the race( as long as if they finished in the top 10 ).
